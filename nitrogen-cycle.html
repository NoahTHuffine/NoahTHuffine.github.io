Nitrogen Cycle & Water Quality

The Nitrogen Cycle

Ammonia → Nitrite → Nitrate

The nitrogen cycle is a natural biological process that keeps aquarium water safe by breaking down harmful waste. Fish produce waste and uneaten food or decaying plants also release ammonia into the water. Ammonia is extremely toxic to fish, even in small amounts. In a healthy aquarium, beneficial bacteria develop in the filter, substrate, and surfaces of the tank. The first type of bacteria converts ammonia into nitrite. Although this removes ammonia, nitrite is still very harmful to fish and can interfere with their ability to carry oxygen in their blood. A second group of beneficial bacteria then converts nitrite into nitrate. Nitrate is much less toxic than ammonia or nitrite, but it can still become harmful if it builds up too much. Nitrate is usually removed through regular water changes, live plants absorbing nutrients, or special filtration methods. This cycle is essential for maintaining stable and safe water conditions in an aquarium.

Tank Cycling

Cycling a tank means establishing the beneficial bacteria needed for the nitrogen cycle before fish are added. When a new aquarium is first set up, it does not yet have the bacteria needed to break down fish waste. If fish are added too early, ammonia and nitrite can quickly rise to dangerous levels and harm or kill the fish. By cycling the tank first, the bacteria colonies are able to grow and become strong enough to process waste safely. This creates a stable biological filtration system that keeps the water healthy. Many aquarists perform a fishless cycle by adding a source of ammonia to the tank to feed the bacteria while no fish are present. This method is considered the safest because it avoids exposing fish to toxic conditions. Once the cycle is complete and tests show zero ammonia and nitrite with some nitrate present, the tank is ready for fish.

Water Testing

Testing water parameters is an important part of maintaining a healthy aquarium. Regular testing allows aquarists to monitor water conditions and detect problems before they harm fish. One important parameter is pH, which measures how acidic or alkaline the water is. Most freshwater fish prefer a pH between about 6.5 and 7.5, although some species require slightly different conditions. Ammonia and nitrite levels should always be zero in a properly cycled aquarium because both substances are highly toxic to fish. Nitrate is less harmful but should generally be kept below about 40 parts per million to prevent stress and algae growth. Water hardness, which includes general hardness (GH) and carbonate hardness (KH), measures the amount of dissolved minerals and affects both fish health and pH stability. Temperature is another key factor, as most tropical fish require stable water temperatures between about 74 and 80 degrees Fahrenheit. These parameters are commonly tested using liquid test kits, test strips, or digital monitoring tools.

Water quality problems can occur in aquariums if maintenance is not consistent or if the tank is not properly balanced. High ammonia levels are often caused by overfeeding, dead fish, decaying plants, or an aquarium that has not fully cycled. This problem can usually be corrected by performing water changes, removing waste, and reducing feeding. High nitrite levels are typically a sign that the nitrogen cycle is not fully established or that the tank is overcrowded. Water changes and allowing more time for beneficial bacteria to grow can help solve this issue. High nitrate levels often develop when water changes are not done regularly or when too much waste accumulates in the tank. Regular maintenance and the addition of live plants can help lower nitrate levels. Other issues, such as unstable pH or cloudy water, may result from poor filtration, sudden chemical changes, or excess organic waste. Careful monitoring, proper filtration, and routine maintenance are key to preventing most water quality problems and keeping fish healthy.
