Aquarium Filtration

How Filters Support the Nitrogen Cycle

Aquarium filters play a major role in supporting the nitrogen cycle by providing a place for beneficial bacteria to grow. These bacteria need oxygen-rich water and surfaces to attach to, which filters provide through materials such as sponges, ceramic rings, or bio balls. As water flows through the filter, ammonia produced by fish waste passes over the bacteria colonies. The bacteria convert ammonia into nitrite and then nitrite into nitrate, completing the biological filtration process. Because filters constantly circulate water through these bacteria colonies, they help maintain stable water chemistry and prevent toxic substances from building up in the aquarium. Without an established filter system, the nitrogen cycle would not function effectively, and harmful compounds could quickly reach dangerous levels.

Types of Filtration

  • Mechanical filtration is the process of physically removing solid waste and debris from the water. As water flows through the filter, materials such as filter pads, sponges, or filter floss trap particles like uneaten food, fish waste, and plant debris. This helps keep the water clear and prevents waste from breaking down and producing harmful chemicals. Mechanical filtration is usually the first stage in most filters because it removes large particles before the water reaches the biological filter media. However, the media must be cleaned regularly to prevent it from becoming clogged and restricting water flow.
  • Biological filtration is the most important type of filtration in an aquarium because it supports the beneficial bacteria that carry out the nitrogen cycle. These bacteria live on surfaces such as sponges, ceramic rings, bio balls, and other porous filter media. They convert toxic ammonia from fish waste into nitrite, and then another group of bacteria converts nitrite into nitrate, which is much less harmful. Biological filtration keeps toxic substances from building up in the tank and is essential for maintaining stable water conditions for fish and other aquatic life.
  • Chemical filtration removes dissolved substances from the water through chemical reactions or absorption. This is usually done using specialized filter media such as activated carbon, resins, or phosphate removers. Activated carbon can remove odors, discoloration, and certain chemicals from the water. Chemical filtration is not always necessary for everyday aquarium operation but can be useful for removing medications, toxins, or unwanted compounds from the water.

Filter Types

  • Canister filters are external filters that sit below the aquarium and pump water through a sealed container filled with multiple layers of filter media. They are very powerful and can hold large amounts of mechanical, biological, and chemical filtration materials. Because of this, they are commonly used on medium to large aquariums or tanks with heavy fish stocking. Canister filters are highly effective but are more expensive than many other filter types and can take longer to clean and maintain.
  • Hang-on-back filters are one of the most common types used in home aquariums. They attach to the back of the tank and pull water up through a tube before running it through filter media and returning it to the aquarium. These filters are easy to install and maintain, which makes them popular for beginners. However, they generally hold less filter media than canister filters and may not be strong enough for very large aquariums.
  • Sponge filters are simple filters powered by an air pump that pulls water through a porous sponge. As water moves through the sponge, debris is trapped and beneficial bacteria grow on the sponge’s surface. Sponge filters provide excellent biological filtration and gentle water movement, making them ideal for breeding tanks, shrimp tanks, or aquariums with small or delicate fish. Their main disadvantage is that they provide limited mechanical filtration compared to more powerful filters.
  • Internal filters sit directly inside the aquarium and use a small electric pump to move water through filter media. They are compact, easy to install, and often used in small aquariums or quarantine tanks. Because they are placed inside the tank, they can take up space and may not provide as much filtration power as larger external filters.
  • Undergravel filters work by pulling water through the substrate at the bottom of the aquarium. Plates are placed under the gravel, and water is drawn down through the substrate and up through lift tubes using either an air pump or powerhead. The gravel acts as a large biological filter because beneficial bacteria grow on its surface. While undergravel filters can provide good biological filtration, they can become clogged with debris over time and are less commonly used in modern aquariums.

Filter Maintenance

Regular filter maintenance is necessary to keep the filtration system working properly. Over time, debris can build up in filter media and reduce water flow, which lowers the efficiency of the filter. Mechanical media such as sponges or filter pads should be rinsed periodically to remove trapped waste. However, it is important to rinse them in aquarium water rather than tap water so that beneficial bacteria are not killed by chlorine. Biological media should be cleaned gently and less frequently because it contains the bacteria that support the nitrogen cycle. Chemical media such as activated carbon usually needs to be replaced regularly because it becomes saturated and stops working over time. Proper maintenance ensures that the filter continues to provide effective mechanical, biological, and chemical filtration while keeping the aquarium ecosystem stable and healthy.
